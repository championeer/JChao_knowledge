# The 5 Services You Can Start Selling This Week

These are not hypothetical. These are services real people are charging real money for right now.

# Service 1: AI Workflow Audits ($500 to $2,000)

This is the easiest entry point and the one I recommend starting with.

You sit down with a business (virtually or in person) and audit their current workflows. You identify every task that could be partially or fully automated with AI. You write up a report showing them exactly what to automate, how much time they would save, and what tools to use.

**What you deliver:** A written report (10 to 20 pages) that maps their current workflows, identifies automation opportunities, estimates time savings, and provides implementation recommendations.

**Why businesses pay for this:** They know AI could help them but they have no idea where to start. Your audit gives them a clear, prioritized roadmap they can act on immediately.

**How to price it:** $500 for a small business with one to three workflows. $1,000 to $2,000 for a mid-size company with five or more workflows. Price based on complexity and the value of time saved.

**How to deliver it:** Spend one to two hours interviewing the client about their daily operations. Ask them to walk you through their most time-consuming recurring tasks. Then spend three to four hours writing the audit using Claude to help you structure the analysis and recommendations.

Total time investment: five to six hours. For $1,000, that is $160 to $200 per hour of your time.

# Service 2: Custom AI System Builds ($1,000 to $5,000)

Once you have done a few audits, clients will naturally ask you to implement the recommendations. This is where the real money starts.

You build the actual AI systems. This could be a custom chatbot for their customer support team. An automated content pipeline for their marketing. A document processing system for their legal team. An email classification and routing system for their operations.

**What you deliver:** A working AI system that automates a specific workflow. This includes the system prompts, the integration, testing, documentation, and a short training session showing them how to use it.

**Why businesses pay for this:** Building AI systems requires knowledge they do not have and do not have time to learn. You turn weeks of their fumbling into days of your expertise.

**How to price it:** $1,000 to $2,000 for simple single-workflow automations (content generation, email templates, document summaries). $3,000 to $5,000 for complex multi-step systems (customer support agents, multi-document analysis, integrated pipelines).

**Pricing rule:** estimate the annual time savings in hours, multiply by what the client pays their employees per hour, and charge 10 to 20 percent of that annual savings. If your system saves 500 hours per year and their employees cost $40 per hour, the annual value is $20,000. Charging $2,000 to $4,000 is an easy yes.

# Service 3: Prompt Engineering and System Prompt Libraries ($500 to $2,000)

Many businesses are already using AI but getting mediocre results because their prompts are terrible.

You come in, audit their existing prompts, rewrite them using proper techniques (context engineering, XML structuring, few-shot examples, negative constraints), and deliver a library of production-quality prompts for their most common tasks.

**What you deliver:** A documented library of 10 to 30 optimized prompts and system prompts, organized by use case, with instructions for each one.

**Why businesses pay for this:** They are already paying for AI subscriptions and getting bad outputs. Better prompts turn their existing investment into something that actually works.

**How to price it:** $500 for a small library (10 prompts for one department). $1,000 to $2,000 for a comprehensive library (20 to 30 prompts across multiple departments).

This is high margin work because the time investment is relatively low once you know what you are doing. A well-structured prompt library can be delivered in a day of focused work.

# Service 4: AI Training and Workshops ($1,000 to $3,000 per session)

Companies know their teams need to learn AI. Most corporate training on this topic is terrible. It is either too basic (here is what ChatGPT is) or too technical (here is how transformers work).

You deliver practical, hands-on workshops that teach teams how to actually use AI for their specific role.

**What you deliver:** A 2 to 4 hour interactive workshop with slides, live demonstrations, hands-on exercises, and take-home resources. Customized for the specific team (marketing, sales, operations, legal, etc.).

**Why businesses pay for this:** Training ten employees for $2,000 is dramatically cheaper than having ten employees waste ten hours each figuring it out on their own. That is 100 hours of salary savings versus your one-time fee.

**How to price it:** $1,000 for a small team (5 to 10 people, 2 hours). $2,000 to $3,000 for a larger team or full-day session.

# Service 5: Monthly AI Retainers ($1,000 to $5,000 per month)

This is the best business model because it generates recurring revenue. You become the company's AI consultant on an ongoing basis.

You handle prompt optimization, workflow improvements, new automations, troubleshooting, and keeping them current on tools and techniques as the field evolves.

**What you deliver:** A set number of hours per month (10 to 20 hours) dedicated to maintaining, improving, and expanding their AI systems. Monthly reports on performance and new opportunities.

**Why businesses pay for this:** AI changes fast. They need someone who stays on top of it so they do not have to. A monthly retainer is cheaper than a full-time hire and gives them access to expertise on demand.

**How to price it:** $1,000 to $2,000 per month for small businesses (10 hours of work). $3,000 to $5,000 per month for mid-size companies (15 to 20 hours of work).

Three retainer clients at $2,000 per month is $6,000 per month recurring. That is $72,000 per year from just three relationships.

# How to Find Your First Clients

This is where most people get stuck. They know what to offer but they do not know how to get someone to say yes.

Here are five approaches that work, ranked by speed:

**Approach 1: Your existing network.** This is the fastest path to revenue. Post on LinkedIn, X, or anywhere your professional network lives. Do not post an advertisement. Post a demonstration.

Show a before and after. "I took a 4-hour weekly report process and automated it down to 15 minutes using Claude. Here is how." Describe the problem, show the solution, mention that you do this as a service.

People in your network who have similar problems will reach out. This is how most AI freelancers get their first clients.

**Approach 2: Local businesses.** Walk into businesses in your area. Law firms, real estate agencies, marketing agencies, accounting firms, medical practices. Ask the owner or manager: "How much time does your team spend on repetitive document work every week?"

Whatever number they say, explain that you can cut it in half with AI. Offer to do a free 30-minute demo. If they are impressed, propose a paid audit.

Local businesses are underserved by AI because most AI freelancers are targeting tech companies. The opportunity is enormous.

**Approach 3: Industry-specific communities.** Join online communities where your target clients hang out. Real estate forums. Legal tech groups. E-commerce seller communities. Marketing Slack channels.

Contribute genuinely for two weeks. Answer questions. Share useful insights. Build credibility. Then mention your services when relevant.

**Approach 4: Cold outreach.** Identify 50 businesses that would benefit from your services. Send each one a personalized message that addresses a specific problem they likely have.

Do not pitch your services directly. Instead, offer a free insight. "I noticed your website FAQ section has 50+ questions. I built a system that turns FAQs into a 24/7 AI customer support agent that handles 80 percent of questions automatically. Would a quick demo be useful?"

**Approach 5: Build in public.** Document your AI projects on X or LinkedIn. Share what you build, what you learn, and what results you achieve. Over time, this attracts clients to you instead of you chasing them.

This is the slowest approach but it compounds. After three months of consistent posting, inbound inquiries start arriving. After six months, you stop needing to do outreach entirely.

# How to Deliver Work That Gets Referrals

Getting the first client is hard. Getting the next ten is easy if the first client loves your work.

**Rule one: over-deliver on the first project.** Your first client is not just a paycheck. They are a case study, a testimonial source, and a referral engine. Do more than they expect. Add extra recommendations they did not ask for. Follow up after delivery to make sure everything works.

**Rule two: document everything.** Every system you build, take screenshots. Write up the before and after. Calculate the time or money saved. This becomes your portfolio. The better your portfolio, the easier every future sale becomes.

**Rule three: ask for referrals explicitly.** After delivering great work, say: "I am glad this worked well. If you know anyone else who has similar problems, I would appreciate an introduction." Most people will not refer you unless you ask. When you ask, most will happily introduce you.

**Rule four: raise your prices after every three clients.** Your first client might pay $500 for an audit. Your fourth client pays $1,000. Your tenth client pays $2,000. This is how every successful freelancer scales. Do not stay at your beginner rate out of insecurity.
